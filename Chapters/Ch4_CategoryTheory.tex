\chapter{A Taste of Category Theory}

\section{What Are We Doing Here?}\label{sec:ch4-intro}
We've spent three chapters unpacking three species ($\Z$, groups, and rings), and by now
we've probably noticed that the same tune keeps playing. Group homomorphisms and ring
homomorphisms look eerily similar; the direct product of two groups looks eerily like the
Cartesian product of two sets; the First Isomorphism Theorem for groups reads like the
same proof as its ring cousin, just with different nouns. Category theory is the language in
which that tune becomes a single melody.

This chapter is a \textit{taste}, not a first course in category theory. We want three
ideas in your hands (\textit{categories}, \textit{functors}, and \textit{natural
transformations}), plus one capstone, \textit{limits}, that ties every universal property we
meet into a single construction. Each idea will land on a MAWF example you already trust,
and if you want to go further,
\href{https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.05316}{Fong and Spivak's \textit{Seven Sketches}}
\cite{fong2018seven} or Roman's \textit{An Introduction to the Language of Category Theory}
\cite{roman2017introduction} are two natural next stops.

Category theory itself was invented in 1945 by
\href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_Eilenberg}{Samuel Eilenberg} and
\href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saunders_Mac_Lane}{Saunders Mac Lane} in a paper titled
\textit{General Theory of Natural Equivalences}. Their actual problem was an annoyance in
algebraic topology, where the same map kept showing up in different disguises and they wanted
to say ``the same'' \textit{precisely}. To define a natural transformation they needed
functors; to define functors they needed categories; and so the language that now reorganizes
large parts of mathematics got built, a bit accidentally, from the top down. We'll walk the
same road, in the same order.

\noindent \textbf{Promises.} By the end of this chapter we will:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\roman*)]
    \item define categories and see that sets, groups, and rings live in them naturally;
    \item use the universal property of products to build the direct product of rings
    \textit{for free}, with no component-wise operation tables required;
    \item meet functors through forgetful functors and product-as-a-functor;
    \item meet natural transformations by comparing two different constructions on the same
    category;
    \item assemble all of the above into the single notion of a \textit{limit}, and watch
    products, equalizers, and even the kernels from Chapter 2 fall out as special cases.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent A friendly warning: Chapter 4 is genuinely more abstract than the chapters before
it. We will manage the abstraction out loud. Whenever we climb the ladder, we'll come back
down and cash out what the climb bought us in terms of things you should by now be quite
familiar with.

Speaking of which: \hyperref[note:ch1-gcd-teaser]{back in Chapter 1 we posed a question to
the CT-aware reader}. Does the definition of $GCD(a,b)$ look familiar? Can you think of a
category in which GCD \textit{is} a categorical product? We are now a few sections away from
being able to answer that, and we will in \S\ref{sec:products}. First, we need categories.

\section{Categories}\label{sec:categories}
Our main object of study is the \textit{category}. Before giving the formal
definition, let's walk through a familiar example informally.

Take the collection of all sets\footnote{The collection of all sets is not itself a
set. If it were, \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russell\%27s_paradox}{Russell's
paradox} would follow. It is a \textit{proper class}. Informally, a \textit{class} is
any collection of objects specified by a property (``all sets,'' ``all groups''), and a
class that is too large to be a set is called \textit{proper}. Every set is a class,
but not every class is a set. This is why our definition says ``collection'' of
objects rather than ``set'': we need it wide enough to include classes that aren't
sets. A category is called \textit{small} if both its collection of objects
and its collection of morphisms form sets; otherwise it is \textit{large}. So
$\mathbf{Set}$, $\mathbf{Grp}$, and $\mathbf{Ring}$ are all large categories, while
the divides poset $\mathbf{DivPos}$ we'll meet shortly is small. All of our categories will be \textit{locally small}: for
any two objects, the morphisms between them form a set, even when the objects
themselves do not. Deeper class-theoretic foundations (NBG, Grothendieck universes,
and the like) are outside this book's scope.} as our \textit{objects}. For any two sets $A$ and $B$,
take the functions from $A$ to $B$ as our \textit{arrows} (or \textit{morphisms}) from
$A$ to $B$. Given $f : A \to B$ and $g : B \to C$, the \textit{composition}
$g \circ f : A \to C$ is defined by $(g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x))$. For each set $A$, the
identity function $\mathrm{id}_A(x) = x$ serves as an \textit{identity arrow} on $A$.
Composition is associative ($((h \circ g) \circ f)(x)$ and $(h \circ (g \circ f))(x)$
both equal $h(g(f(x)))$, by Lemma~\ref{lem:assoc-comp}, the Associative Law of
Composition), and identities behave the way identities should:
$f \circ \mathrm{id}_A = f$ and $\mathrm{id}_B \circ f = f$ for any $f : A \to B$.

That collection of data (objects, arrows, a composition rule, identities, plus the two
natural laws they satisfy) is the category $\mathbf{Set}$. Strip away the particular
words ``sets'' and ``functions'' and keep only the shape of the data, and you get the
following definition.

\begin{definition}[Category]
A \textit{category} $\mathcal{C}$ consists of:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\roman*)]
    \item a collection of \textit{objects}, written $\mathrm{ob}(\mathcal{C})$;
    \item for each pair of objects $A, B \in \mathrm{ob}(\mathcal{C})$, a collection
    of \textit{morphisms} (or \textit{arrows}) from $A$ to $B$, written
    $\mathrm{Hom}_\mathcal{C}(A, B)$ (or just $\mathrm{Hom}(A, B)$ when unambiguous);
    we write $f : A \to B$ to mean $f \in \mathrm{Hom}(A, B)$;
    \item for each composable pair $f : A \to B$ and $g : B \to C$, a
    \textit{composite} $g \circ f : A \to C$;
    \item for each object $A$, an \textit{identity morphism}
    $\mathrm{id}_A : A \to A$;
\end{enumerate}
satisfying the two axioms:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item (\textit{Associativity.}) For all composable triples
    $f : A \to B$, $g : B \to C$, $h : C \to D$,
    \[ (h \circ g) \circ f \; = \; h \circ (g \circ f). \]
    \item (\textit{Identity.}) For every $f : A \to B$,
    \[ f \circ \mathrm{id}_A \; = \; f \; = \; \mathrm{id}_B \circ f. \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\noindent We'll use ``morphism'' in formal statements and ``arrow'' informally,
especially when drawing diagrams or cashing abstract claims back out in a concrete
category.

\begin{remark}
A first guess at what a category is: take a group and drop the requirement that
every element have an inverse. That's close, but not quite right, and the difference
matters.

In a group, the operation is \textit{total}: any two elements multiply. Our
Chapter 2 definition spells this out as a separate axiom called
\textit{closure}. A more modern, equivalent convention treats the binary
operation as a function $\cdot : G \times G \to G$, in which case closure is
automatic: every pair in $G \times G$ lands somewhere in $G$ because that's
what functions do. Either way, $gh$ is in $G$, no questions asked.

In a category, composition is \textit{partial}. The composite $g \circ f$ exists
\textit{only when} the target of $f$ matches the source of $g$. Every morphism
carries a source and a target object, and before asking ``what is the composite?''
you have to ask ``is there one?'' Composition is governed by a \textit{typing
discipline}: the objects tell you which pairs of arrows can compose.

So categories are not groups with the inverse axiom removed. (That object is called a
\textit{monoid}, and we'll meet it shortly.) A group is the very degenerate case of
a category with exactly one object, where every pair of arrows composes because there
is only one source and one target available, \textit{and} every arrow is invertible.
Most categories are not like that.
\end{remark}

\noindent Now let's verify that the familiar species from Chapters 1--3 are categories in
this formal sense. A convention first: throughout Chapter 4, ``ring'' means a
commutative unital ring, and we strengthen Chapter 3's ring-homomorphism definition
slightly. For Chapter 4, a \textit{ring homomorphism} $\varphi : R \to R'$ preserves
$+$ and $\cdot$ (as in Definition~\ref{def:ring-hom}) \textit{and} the multiplicative
identity: $\varphi(1_R) = 1_{R'}$.\footnote{The one Chapter 3 example this excludes
is the trivial map $\varphi(a) = 0'$, which sends $1_R$ to $0'$. All other
Chapter 3 ring-homs preserve the unit, but for two different reasons: the surjective
ones (identity, complex conjugation, quotient projection, evaluation) by a clean
general argument, and the embeddings ($\Z \hookrightarrow \Q$, field of fractions)
by explicit construction (they are defined to send $1 \mapsto 1$). The surjective
case is the clean one: let $e = \varphi(1_R)$; for any $r' \in R'$, surjectivity
gives $r \in R$ with $\varphi(r) = r'$, and then
$e \cdot r' = \varphi(1_R)\varphi(r) = \varphi(1_R \cdot r) = \varphi(r) = r'$
(symmetrically on the other side), so $e$ is a two-sided multiplicative identity
in $R'$ and $e = 1_{R'}$ by uniqueness.} This matches the commutative-unital
setting Chapter 3 settled into and keeps the categorical product story in \S\ref{sec:products} clean.

\begin{example}[The category $\mathbf{Set}$]
The informal walkthrough at the start of this section was already the verification.
Objects are sets, morphisms are functions, composition is function composition,
identities are identity functions, associativity is Lemma~\ref{lem:assoc-comp}, and
the identity laws are immediate. So $\mathbf{Set}$ is a category.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[The category $\mathbf{Grp}$]
Objects are groups. A morphism from $G$ to $H$ is a group homomorphism
$\varphi : G \to H$ (Definition~\ref{def:group-hom}). The composite of two
homomorphisms is again a homomorphism (we worked this out in Chapter 2 as part
of showing isomorphism is an equivalence relation, Proposition~\ref{prop:iso-equiv-rel},
transitivity step): if $\varphi : G \to H$ and $\psi : H \to K$ both respect the
group operation, so does $\psi \circ \varphi$. Identity functions are
homomorphisms (trivially), and associativity of composition is again
Lemma~\ref{lem:assoc-comp}. So $\mathbf{Grp}$ is a category.

Back in Chapter 2 we said informally that a homomorphism is a map that
\textit{splits over products}, meaning $\varphi(ab) = \varphi(a)\varphi(b)$. That
phrase is about to become much more interesting. In \S\ref{sec:products} we will meet a
categorical notion of ``product,'' and then ``splits over products'' will turn out
to be a literal statement about how $\varphi$ interacts with a universal property.
For now, file the phrase away.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}
Here is a twist worth a moment. We just said the objects of $\mathbf{Grp}$ are
\textit{groups}, plural, with homomorphisms between them. But a single group $G$,
on its own, is also a category, in a rather different sense: take one formal
object $*$, declare the morphisms $* \to *$ to be the elements of $G$, take the
group operation as composition, and take the identity element $e \in G$ as the
identity morphism. Every morphism is invertible, because every element of $G$ is.
This is \textit{not} the same category as $\mathbf{Grp}$. $\mathbf{Grp}$ has many
objects, with homomorphisms between them; the group-as-a-category has exactly one
object, and its morphisms \textit{are} the group's elements.

The perspective shift is worth a meditation. Morphisms here are not functions
between groups; they are \textit{elements of} $G$, and composition is literally
the group operation.\footnote{If you are itching to see this as function
composition, identify each $g \in G$ with the left-multiplication map
$L_g : G \to G$ given by $L_g(x) = gx$. Then $(L_g \circ L_h)(x) = g(hx) = (gh)x
= L_{gh}(x)$, so $L_g \circ L_h = L_{gh}$: morphism composition matches the
group operation in the expected order. This is essentially Cayley's Theorem from
Chapter 2 in categorical language. A function-realization like this
isn't always available, though, as the next example will show. Part of getting
comfortable with categories is being willing to cast whatever structures are at
hand, even unexpected or non-function-like ones, into the roles of object,
morphism, composition, and identity.} We will see this whole move generalize in a
moment when we meet monoids.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[The category $\mathbf{Ring}$]
Objects are (commutative unital) rings, with ring homomorphisms as morphisms in the
sense of our Chapter 4 convention above. The composite of two such maps is again a
ring homomorphism, by the same trick we used for groups applied to each of the two
ring operations; we leave the verification to the reader as a brief exercise.
Unit-preservation chains directly:
$\psi(\varphi(1_R)) = \psi(1_{R'}) = 1_{R''}$. Identity functions are ring
homomorphisms, and associativity is inherited from function composition. So
$\mathbf{Ring}$ is a category.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[The category $(\Z, \leq)$]
Objects are integers. For each pair $a, b$, there is exactly \textit{one}
morphism $a \to b$ if $a \leq b$, and \textit{no} morphism otherwise (so
$\mathrm{Hom}(a,b)$ is a one-element set when $a \leq b$, and empty otherwise).
Composition is transitivity of $\leq$: given the morphism $a \to b$ and the
morphism $b \to c$, the composite is the morphism $a \to c$, which exists
precisely because $a \leq b$ and $b \leq c$ imply $a \leq c$. The identity
morphism on $a$ is the arrow $a \to a$ given by reflexivity, $a \leq a$.
Associativity is automatic: between any two integers there is at most one arrow,
so any two composites with the same source and target are equal.

This one is worth pausing on. The morphisms here are \textit{not} functions,
and composition is \textit{not} function composition. A morphism is a witness\footnote{We
borrow the word \textit{witness} from logic: an object whose existence
certifies a claim. The morphism $a \to b$ in $(\Z, \leq)$ exists precisely
when $a \leq b$, so its mere presence records the truth of that fact. There
is no further data attached to the morphism beyond that, which is why
$\mathrm{Hom}(a, b)$ is a one-element set when $a \leq b$ and empty
otherwise.} to the fact ``$a \leq b$''; composition is the statement
``$\leq$ is transitive'' packaged as a category law; identity is the
statement ``$\leq$ is reflexive.''
This is the first hint that ``category'' reaches well past ``collections of sets
with structure and structure-preserving functions between them.''
\end{example}

\begin{definition}[Partially Ordered Set]\label{def:poset}
A \textit{partially ordered set}\index{Partially Ordered Set} (or
\textit{poset}\index{Poset}) is a set $X$ equipped with a binary relation
$\leq$ on $X$ that is:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\roman*)]
    \item \textit{reflexive}: $x \leq x$ for all $x \in X$;
    \item \textit{antisymmetric}: $x \leq y$ and $y \leq x$ imply $x = y$;
    \item \textit{transitive}: $x \leq y$ and $y \leq z$ imply $x \leq z$.
\end{enumerate}
Two elements $x, y \in X$ are \textit{incomparable}\index{Incomparable
elements} if neither $x \leq y$ nor $y \leq x$.
\end{definition}

\noindent The example $(\Z, \leq)$ above is the most familiar poset around: the
integers under their usual order, with reflexivity, antisymmetry, and transitivity
all standard. In fact $(\Z, \leq)$ has a stronger property: any two integers are
comparable, making it a \textit{total order}\index{Total Order}. But the poset
axioms are all the construction below needs. (Dropping antisymmetry from the
three axioms instead would give the weaker notion of a
\textit{preorder}\index{Preorder}.) More generally, every
poset $(X, \leq)$ gives rise to a category in the same way that $(\Z, \leq)$
did: objects are elements of $X$, and there is a unique morphism $x \to y$
whenever $x \leq y$. Composition is the transitivity of $\leq$, identity is
reflexivity, and associativity is free since between any two objects there is
at most one arrow. The next example takes a less familiar poset (divisibility
on the positive integers) and runs the same construction.

\begin{example}[The category $\mathbf{DivPos}$]
First, as a poset: take $X = \Z^+$ with $a \leq b$ defined to mean ``$a$ divides
$b$'' (Definition~\ref{def:divides}). Reflexivity is immediate: $a \mid a$ since
$a = 1 \cdot a$. Transitivity: if $a \mid b$ and $b \mid c$, write $b = ma$ and
$c = nb$, so $c = (nm)a$ and $a \mid c$. Antisymmetry: if $a \mid b$ and
$b \mid a$ on $\Z^+$, write $b = ma$ and $a = nb$, substitute to get
$a = (nm)a$, so $nm = 1$ and hence $m = n = 1$, forcing $a = b$. (Pairs of
positive integers where neither divides the other, like $4$ and $6$, are
incomparable in this poset.)

Now apply the poset-to-category construction above. Objects are positive
integers; morphisms are divisibility statements; composition is transitivity;
identities are reflexivity. So $\mathbf{DivPos}$ is a category.

If the category feels strange, recall that an arrow $a \to b$ is the statement
that $a$ divides $b$. Composing the arrow $2 \to 6$ with the arrow $6 \to 12$ is
the statement ``$2$ divides $6$ and $6$ divides $12$, so $2$ divides $12$,''
which is something you already knew. The category is just bookkeeping for
divisibility.

This is the category Chapter 1 was teasing back in the GCD aside, and we will
answer the teaser fully in \S\ref{sec:products} when we meet the categorical product.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}
The group-as-a-category trick we just saw has a wider reach. Drop the
requirement that every arrow be invertible, and what remains of a one-object
category is still a perfectly good category: a single object $*$, a collection
of arrows $* \to *$, an associative composition rule, and a two-sided identity
arrow. The structure on the arrows alone is a set with an associative binary
operation and a two-sided identity element. Such a thing has a name in algebra:
it is called a \textit{monoid}\index{Monoid}. So a category with exactly one object is the
same data as a monoid, and a group is precisely a monoid in which every element
has an inverse, equivalently, a one-object category in which every arrow is
invertible. We will see this correspondence sharpen in \S\ref{sec:functors}, when functors
will let us read ``monoid homomorphism'' off the categorical structure for free.
\end{remark}

\noindent A curious reader might ask: are rings one-object categories too? Almost,
but not cleanly. Taking the elements of a ring $R$ as morphisms $* \to *$ under
multiplication recovers the multiplicative monoid of $R$, but the additive
structure goes missing entirely. Capturing both operations categorically needs
the machinery of \textit{enriched categories} (specifically,
$\mathbf{Ab}$-enriched categories, where the morphism collections themselves
carry abelian-group structure). That is past our scope.

\begin{definition}[Categorical Isomorphism]\label{def:cat-iso}
Let $\mathcal{C}$ be a category. A morphism $f : A \to B$ in $\mathcal{C}$ is an
\textit{isomorphism}\index{Isomorphism!Categorical}
if there exists a morphism $g : B \to A$ such that
$g \circ f = \mathrm{id}_A$ and $f \circ g = \mathrm{id}_B$. We say $A$ and $B$
are \textit{isomorphic}, written $A \cong B$, if such a pair $(f, g)$ exists.
\end{definition}

\noindent (Chapters 2 and 3 wrote group and ring isomorphism as $A \approx B$;
in this chapter we switch to $A \cong B$ to match standard category-theory
notation. The two symbols mean the same thing in $\mathbf{Grp}$ and
$\mathbf{Ring}$, as the reader can verify by comparing the categorical
definition above with Definition~\ref{def:group-iso} (group isomorphism) and
Definition~\ref{def:ring-iso} (ring isomorphism).)

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:iso-equiv-cat}

    In any category $\mathcal{C}$, the relation $\cong$ is an equivalence relation
on $\mathrm{ob}(\mathcal{C})$.

\noindent \textit{Proof.} \textit{Reflexivity:} $A \cong A$ via the pair
$(\mathrm{id}_A, \mathrm{id}_A)$, since
$\mathrm{id}_A \circ \mathrm{id}_A = \mathrm{id}_A$ by the identity axiom.
\textit{Symmetry:} if $A \cong B$ via $(f, g)$, then $B \cong A$ via $(g, f)$,
since the same two equations $g \circ f = \mathrm{id}_A$ and
$f \circ g = \mathrm{id}_B$ now witness $g$ as an isomorphism with inverse $f$.
\textit{Transitivity:} if $A \cong B$ via $(f, g)$ and $B \cong C$ via $(h, k)$,
then $A \cong C$ via $(h \circ f, g \circ k)$. Indeed,
\[
    (g \circ k) \circ (h \circ f) = g \circ (k \circ h) \circ f
    = g \circ \mathrm{id}_B \circ f = g \circ f = \mathrm{id}_A,
\]
and the equation $(h \circ f) \circ (g \circ k) = \mathrm{id}_C$ follows by the
symmetric calculation. $\blacksquare$
\end{proposition}

\noindent This generalizes Chapter 2's Proposition~\ref{prop:iso-equiv-rel}, which
proved the same thing for the special case $\mathcal{C} = \mathbf{Grp}$. The
categorical proof is shorter and uses nothing beyond the category axioms.

\noindent The definition lifts cleanly to every category we have seen, and the cash-out
is exactly what one would hope. In $\mathbf{Set}$, an isomorphism is a bijection;
since sets carry no structure for morphisms to preserve in the first place,
``$A \cong B$ in $\mathbf{Set}$'' bottoms out at ``$A$ and $B$ have the same
cardinality.'' In $\mathbf{Grp}$, it is the bijective homomorphism notion from
Definition~\ref{def:group-iso}. In $\mathbf{Ring}$, it is the analogous bijective
unital ring homomorphism. In $\mathbf{DivPos}$ and $(\Z, \leq)$, however, the
notion collapses to equality. An isomorphism $a \to b$ requires arrows in both
directions, which means $a \leq b$ and $b \leq a$ in the underlying poset, and
antisymmetry then forces $a = b$. So in poset categories, ``isomorphic'' just
means ``equal,'' which will matter later when we say constructions are ``unique
up to unique isomorphism.''

\begin{example}[A bijective morphism that is not an isomorphism, after Roman]
Outside the comfort of $\mathbf{Set}$, $\mathbf{Grp}$, $\mathbf{Ring}$, and the
like, ``bijective morphism'' and ``isomorphism'' can come apart. Roman
\cite{roman2017introduction} gives a clean illustration. Consider the category
whose objects are posets and whose morphisms are monotone (order-preserving)
maps. Take the poset $P = \{a, b\}$ in which $a$ and $b$ are incomparable, and
the poset $Q = \{0, 1\}$ with $0 < 1$. Define $f : P \to Q$ by $f(a) = 0$ and
$f(b) = 1$.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[
    point/.style={circle, fill=black, inner sep=1.5pt},
    setblob/.style={draw=gray!60, fill=gray!12, thick},
    scale=0.95
]
    % P container (horizontal ellipse)
    \draw[setblob] (0.7, 0) ellipse (1.25 and 0.65);
    \node[font=\itshape, anchor=north] at (0.7, -0.75) {P};

    % P points
    \node[point, label=below:{$a$}] (a) at (0,0) {};
    \node[point, label=below:{$b$}] (b) at (1.4,0) {};

    % Q container (vertical ellipse)
    \draw[setblob] (4.5, 0.7) ellipse (0.65 and 1.25);
    \node[font=\itshape, anchor=north] at (4.5, -0.75) {Q};

    % Q points
    \node[point, label=right:{$0$}] (q0) at (4.5,0) {};
    \node[point, label=right:{$1$}] (q1) at (4.5,1.4) {};
    \draw[->] (q0) -- (q1);

    % f arrows
    \draw[->, dashed] (a) to[bend right=42] node[midway, below, font=\footnotesize]{$f$} (q0);
    \draw[->, dashed] (b) to[bend left=10] node[midway, above, font=\footnotesize]{$f$} (q1);
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

As a function, $f$ is a bijection; and since $P$ has no nontrivial order
relations to preserve, $f$ is monotone vacuously. So $f$ is a bijective
morphism.

But $f$ is not an isomorphism. The would-be inverse $g : Q \to P$ has to send
$0 \mapsto a$ and $1 \mapsto b$ as a function. For $g$ to be a morphism in our
category, $g$ must itself be monotone, which requires $g(0) \leq g(1)$ in $P$,
i.e., $a \leq b$. But $a$ and $b$ are incomparable in $P$, so $g$ is not
monotone, hence not a morphism. The set-theoretic inverse exists; the categorical
inverse does not.

This is a good moment to look back. Chapters 2 and 3 defined isomorphism (of
groups, of rings) as a \textit{bijective homomorphism}. Two ingredients did
separate work there: \textit{homomorphism} carried the structure-preservation,
and \textit{bijection} carried the undoability. The categorical definition
folds both into a single requirement. A morphism already preserves whatever
structure its category cares about, by design (the category itself decides
what counts as ``structure-preserving''), so the categorical definition only
has to add the undo: an inverse \textit{morphism}, not just an inverse function.
In $\mathbf{Set}$, $\mathbf{Grp}$, $\mathbf{Ring}$, and similar categories the
two formulations coincide because every set-theoretic inverse of a bijective
morphism happens to itself be a morphism. Outside those, only the categorical
definition captures sameness as we actually mean it. The diagram above makes it
visually obvious that $P$ and $Q$ are not ``the same'' poset; only $Q$ has an
order relation, and the categorical notion of isomorphism is what registers that
distinction. The bijective-morphism notion would mislead us into calling them
the same.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[A two-object diagram, after Magurn]
An early example from \cite{magurn2002algebraic} gives a category that is small
enough to draw entirely:
% quiver source: TBD (render in q.uiver.app and update URL on next polish)
\begin{center}
\begin{tikzcd}[column sep=large]
    A \arrow["\mathrm{id}_A", loop left, distance=2em] \arrow[r, bend left, "f"]
    & B \arrow["\mathrm{id}_B", loop right, distance=2em] \arrow[l, bend left, "g"']
\end{tikzcd}
\end{center}
Two objects $A, B$, two identity morphisms (drawn as loops), and two arrows
$f : A \to B$ and $g : B \to A$. Nothing else. The composition axiom now forces
a striking conclusion. The composite $g \circ f$ is a morphism $A \to A$, but
the only morphism $A \to A$ in the diagram is $\mathrm{id}_A$, so
$g \circ f = \mathrm{id}_A$. Similarly $f \circ g = \mathrm{id}_B$. So $f$ and
$g$ are mutual inverses, and $A \cong B$ falls out of the diagram before we have
written down a single set or function.

The point: a category is specified by its objects, morphisms, and composition
rule. ``No other arrows'' is real data, not artwork. Saying the diagram is
complete already encodes the conclusion that $A$ and $B$ are isomorphic.
\end{example}

\noindent Five categories now sit on our worktable: $\mathbf{Set}$,
$\mathbf{Grp}$, $\mathbf{Ring}$, $(\Z, \leq)$, and $\mathbf{DivPos}$. The first
three repackage species we spent three chapters building. The last two are the
first hint that ``category'' is more flexible than ``a collection of things with
structure.'' Before we do anything ambitious, we will warm up in $\mathbf{Set}$,
where every morphism is a function and intuition can carry as much of the
weight as we want. $\mathbf{Set}$ is Chapter 4's home category, the way $\Z$ was
Chapter 1's home ring-in-waiting.
